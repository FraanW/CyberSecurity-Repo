SAML 2.0 · The gatekeeper's second volume

A signed note, carried by the browser, that opens every door at once.

SAML is old (2005), verbose (XML), and full of sharp edges — but underneath it does one elegant thing: an Identity Provider vouches for you once, and every Service Provider that trusts it lets you in without a password. This is that mechanism, taken apart piece by piece — the two flows, the assertion, the certificates, and exactly what is and isn't encrypted. Then a question bank to prove you own it.

Mode I · starts at the app
SP-initiated

You hit a URL, the app has no session, and it sends you to the IdP with an AuthnRequest. The response is bound to that request. The default — ~95% of logins.

Mode II · starts at the IdP
IdP-initiated

You're already in the IdP portal and click an app tile. The IdP pushes an unsolicited response — no AuthnRequest, no binding. Great for onboarding & testing, weaker for security.

Two faces, like the god of doorways — the same protocol looking out from the app, or in from the portal.

The words people blur in meetings

SSO is the experience. Federation is the machinery.

Get this distinction right and half of SAML falls into place. SAML is one common way to deliver federated SSO — SSO that reaches across company boundaries.

Single Sign-OnLog in once, reach many

One authentication, then every app opens without a new prompt. Says nothing about how — a single company can do it with one shared cookie.

FederationCross-domain trust

Two different security domains agree to trust each other's logins, anchored by a signed contract of metadata + certificates exchanged up front.

Federated SSOThe two, combined

SSO achieved across the boundary via SAML/OIDC — one corporate login opens 40 third-party SaaS apps that never saw your password.

⚿

The memory hook: all federated SSO is SSO; not all SSO is federated. The moment the apps live in someone else's security domain — a SaaS vendor, a partner bank — you need federation. That's exactly the problem SAML was built to solve.

Who's who — and the one name that identifies everybody

Three actors, and the EntityID's three homes

Every SAML message passes between three parties, through the browser. And every party has one globally-unique name — its EntityID — that shows up in exactly three load-bearing places.

The userPrincipal / Subject

The human trying to reach an app. Identified inside the assertion by the NameID.

The IdPIdentity Provider

Authenticates the user and issues the signed assertion. Ping, Okta, Entra, Keycloak, ADFS.

The SPService Provider

The app the user wants. Trusts the IdP's assertion instead of asking for a password.

Where you see itWhose EntityIDWhat it means
AuthnRequest <Issuer>the SP's"This login request is from me, app X" — the IdP looks up the right connection
Response / Assertion <Issuer>the IdP's"I minted this" — the SP uses it to pick which certificate to verify against
<Audience>the SP's"This assertion is only for app X" — stops replay to a different app

Speed-read trick: Issuer answers who sent this; Audience answers who may consume this. It's just a name — usually a URL, but it need not resolve to anything.

The ticket when it's wrong

Two jobs, two keypairs, one flip

Certificates: signing proves it; encryption hides it

The part everyone gets backwards. Signing and encryption use different keypairs, and the key direction flips between them.

Job 1 · Signing

Authenticity + integrity

The IdP signs the assertion with its own private key. The SP verifies with the IdP's public certificate (pinned from metadata). Any changed byte → hash mismatch → rejected.

Proves it's really from this IdP and unmodified. Does not hide anything — a signed assertion is fully readable.

🔑 uses the IdP's keypair
Job 2 · Encryption

Confidentiality

The IdP encrypts using the SP's public certificate; only the SP's private key can decrypt. A random AES key encrypts the XML; that key is wrapped with the SP's RSA key.

Hides the assertion's contents from the browser and anything in between. Optional — off by default.

🔒 uses the SP's keypair
↹

The mnemonic: you sign with your own hand; you encrypt for the reader — with the reader's lock. On the way out the IdP signs then encrypts; on the way in the SP decrypts then verifies. And SAML trust isn't a CA chain — it's the pinned cert from metadata, which is why cert rotation is a scheduled, primary-plus-secondary operation, not an automatic one.

The layer question your senior saves for last

What's encrypted, and what's just… readable

Three independent layers. Confuse them and you'll either panic at a normal assertion or miss a real exposure.

Layer 1 · TLS (transport)

Encrypts the pipe, not the parcel

Every hop is HTTPS, so on the wire everything is encrypted — but TLS terminates at each endpoint (decrypted at the browser, re-encrypted onward). Protects transit; protects nothing once it's sitting in the browser.

Layer 2 · XML signature (integrity)

Not encryption at all

A signed assertion is fully readable — the signature only makes tampering detectable. Like a hologram sticker on a transparent envelope.

Layer 3 · XML encryption (confidentiality)

The only thing that hides content from the browser

Optional. <EncryptedAssertion> hides the whole assertion; <EncryptedID> / <EncryptedAttribute> hide parts. There is no EncryptedResponse — the envelope stays readable so the SP can route and read Status first.

Say this and watch the senior nod: base64 is encoding, not encryption. Anyone who captures a SAML Response — SAML-tracer, devtools, a proxy log — reads it in full, unless the assertion itself was <EncryptedAssertion>.

The 60-second read (tape it to your monitor)

  • Status — success, or which side's error?
  • Issuer — which IdP minted this?
  • InResponseTo — present (SP-init) or absent (IdP-init)?
  • NameID — who, and in what format?
  • Audience — for this SP? Recipient — my ACS?
  • NotBefore / NotOnOrAfter — in window? (else clock skew)
  • Signature — signed at which level, against which cert?
  • Attributes — does the app get what it needs?
