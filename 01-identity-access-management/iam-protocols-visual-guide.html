FINCO · IAM · Identity & Access Management, from the threshold

Every access decision is a gate. Janus watches both sides of it.

The Roman god of doorways had two faces — one looking out, one looking in. So does identity. Every IAM protocol you'll meet at work is a refinement of two questions asked at a gate. Learn to read the gate, and SAML, OIDC, OAuth, and the directory stop being noise.

Face I · Authentication
Who are you?

Proving identity — a password, an MFA factor, a signed assertion. The IdP's job. Get it wrong and an impostor walks in.

Face II · Authorization
What may you do?

Granting access — roles, scopes, group claims. The app's job, using what the IdP vouched for. Get it wrong and a valid user over-reaches.

01 The mental model

Three letters that organize the whole domain: A · A · A

Whenever a protocol name flies past you, ask one reflex question first: is this about authN, authZ, or the directory that backs both? Half of all IAM confusion is mixing them up.

AuthenticationWho are you?

Proving the claim of identity. Factors (know / have / are), MFA, and — across company boundaries — a signed assertion or token.

  • SAML · OIDC do this across trust boundaries
  • Fails → impostor gets in

AuthorizationWhat may you do?

Deciding what an authenticated subject is allowed to touch. Roles (RBAC), attributes (ABAC), scopes, group claims.

  • OAuth 2.0 delegates this to apps
  • Fails → valid user over-reaches

AccountingWhat did you do?

Logging every answer to the first two. In a fintech this is the audit trail your SOX / PCI reviewers live on.

  • SIEM, sign-in logs, session records
  • "The AI told me to" is not a control
02 The idea behind SAML and OIDC

Federation is a passport agreement between gates

The single most important concept in the job. Once the analogy clicks, both federation protocols make sense at once.

The passport analogyWhy the border trusts your papers

A border agent doesn't investigate your birth certificate. They trust a passport issued by your home country's passport office — because countries agreed in advance to trust each other's passports and exchanged the security features to verify them.

The same thing, in identity

An app (the Service Provider) trusts a signed statement from your Identity Provider (Okta, Entra, Keycloak) instead of asking for your password. Trust is anchored in a certificate exchanged during setup — the "hologram."

Passport worldIdentity worldWhat it really is
Passport officeIdentity Provider (IdP)Okta · Entra ID · Keycloak · Ping — vouches for you
Foreign borderService Provider (SP)Salesforce · Workday · your internal app — trusts the vouch
The stamped passportAssertion / TokenA signed statement: "this is Farhaan, authenticated 10:03"
Countries agreeingEstablishing federationExchanging metadata + signing certs up front
Checking the hologramVerifying the signatureThe SP validates against the IdP's certificate
⚿

Why it's a security control, not just convenience: without federation, 100 apps means 100 copies of your password. With it, there's one strong identity at the IdP — disable the person there, and they lose access everywhere at once. That's why your Joiner/Mover/Leaver work matters.

Don't confuse themSSO vs Federation

SSO is the experience — “I logged in once and reached everything.” Federation (SAML/OIDC) is the machinery that delivers it. SSO is the result; federation is one common way to get it.

The engine of SSOA live session at the IdP

The second app skips the password prompt because the IdP still holds your session from the first login. Kill that session and the next app asks again. That's all SSO really is.

04 The tunnel everything rides in

HTTP → HTTPS → mTLS

SAML assertions, OIDC tokens, LDAP binds — they all travel inside a TLS tunnel. Here's the ladder from plaintext to mutual authentication. Step through the handshake in the “TLS + mTLS” tab above.

HTTPPlaintext

Readable and modifiable by anyone on the network path. No encryption, no proof of who the server is.

  • Confidentiality — none
  • Integrity — none
  • Authentication — none

HTTPS = HTTP + TLSServer-authenticated

An encrypted tunnel where the server proves its identity with a CA-signed certificate. This is the padlock.

  • Confidentiality ✓
  • Integrity ✓
  • Server proven · client anonymous

mTLSMutually authenticated

Both sides present certificates. Machine-to-machine authentication — the certificate is the identity.

  • Everything HTTPS has ✓
  • + client proven ✓
  • A Zero-Trust building block

What your manager meant: pods “using HTTP because mTLS is there”

In Kubernetes, a service mesh (Istio, Linkerd) puts a sidecar proxy beside each app. The app speaks plain HTTP to its own sidecar over localhost — that traffic never leaves the pod. The sidecars then speak mTLS to each other on the wire. So the app “uses HTTP,” while the real pod-to-pod traffic is encrypted and mutually authenticated — automatically, with certificates the mesh issues and rotates for you.

The sharp question to ask: is our mesh mTLS in STRICT mode (plaintext to a pod is rejected) or PERMISSIVE mode (plaintext still allowed during migration)? STRICT means it's actually enforced. The nuance to hold: the “HTTP” is app ↔ its own sidecar inside the pod; the wire between pods is mTLS, never plaintext.

mTLS = mutual, certificate-based authentication — the machine-to-machine sibling of the user logins in the other tabs. Full write-up: note 06 in your repo.

06 The decision you'll be asked in design reviews

Which protocol, when — and the one trap

Four protocols, four jobs. The trap: people say "we log in with OAuth." OAuth is authorization, not authentication — the login answer is OIDC (which uses OAuth underneath). Saying that precisely marks you out.

SAML 2.0 · authNEnterprise SSO

Log a user into a legacy/enterprise SaaS (Workday, older SAP, B2B apps). XML, 2005, browser-carried. What enterprise apps standardized on.

OIDC · authNModern login

Log a user into a modern web / mobile / SPA app. JSON & JWT, mobile-friendly, discovery-based. The successor to SAML.

OAuth 2.0 · authZDelegated API access

Let an app call an API on the user's behalf, or service-to-service (client credentials). Issues access tokens — not a login protocol.

LDAP · directoryWhere users live

Look up / store users, groups, org structure — usually against AD or Entra. The source of truth the IdP checks against.
